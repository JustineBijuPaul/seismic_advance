Analysis Guide

P-Waves (Primary Waves)

P-waves are the fastest seismic waves and arrive first at recording stations. They are compressional waves that can travel through both solid and liquid materials.

  • Speed: 6-8 km/s in the Earth's crust
  • Motion: Particles move parallel to wave direction
  • Detection: First clear arrival on seismograms
  • Importance: Used for initial earthquake detection and location

S-Waves (Secondary Waves)

S-waves are slower than P-waves and cause more ground shaking. They cannot travel through liquids, making them important for studying Earth's interior structure.

  • Speed: 3-4 km/s in the Earth's crust
  • Motion: Particles move perpendicular to wave direction
  • Detection: Larger amplitude, arrives after P-waves
  • Importance: Critical for magnitude estimation and damage assessment

Surface Waves

Surface waves travel along the Earth's surface and cause the most damage during earthquakes. They include Love waves and Rayleigh waves.

  • Love Waves: Horizontal shearing motion
  • Rayleigh Waves: Elliptical rolling motion
  • Speed: Slower than body waves (2-4 km/s)
  • Importance: Dominant in earthquake damage, used for magnitude calculation

Wave Analysis Guide

Understanding seismic wave analysis helps interpret earthquake characteristics and assess potential hazards.

  • S-P Time: Difference between S and P arrival times indicates distance to earthquake
  • Amplitude: Wave height relates to earthquake magnitude and energy
  • Frequency: Wave frequency content provides information about source and path
  • Quality Metrics: Signal-to-noise ratio and confidence scores indicate reliability
